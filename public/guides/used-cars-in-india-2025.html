How to inspect and verify a used car before buying

Perform a systematic inspection or hire a professional. Focus areas below reduce the most common post‑purchase surprises.

Exterior & body

Check for uneven paint, misaligned panels, rust spots and evidence of frame repairs — these can indicate accident repairs or poor maintenance.

Engine & mechanicals

Listen for unusual noises, check for oil/water leaks, inspect belts, and verify the engine‑check lights during startup.

Suspension, steering & brakes

Drive on mixed roads to detect knocks, judder, or pulling; check tyre wear and brake response.

Interior & electronics

Inspect seat wear, HVAC operation, infotainment and electrical switches; musty smells can indicate flood damage.

Documents & service history

Verify RC, insurance, number of owners, PUC, and any loan hypothecation status — cross‑check chassis & engine numbers against documents.

Detecting commercial/taxi usage

Look for heavy flooring wear, aftermarket seating or meter mounts, frequent ownership changes and high short‑term mileage spikes — these often signal fleet use.

Financing, warranty & certified programs — reducing buying risk

Certified programs bundle inspection, limited warranty and return windows. Used‑car loans (from banks & NBFCs) are widely available but may have higher rates than new‑car loans.

When certification is worth paying extra

For high‑value purchases or when resale is a priority, paying for certification and warranty often saves money and stress in the medium term.
