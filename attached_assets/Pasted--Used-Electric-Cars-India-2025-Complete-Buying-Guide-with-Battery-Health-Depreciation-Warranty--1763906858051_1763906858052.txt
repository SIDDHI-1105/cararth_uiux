## EV Battery Degradation Reality: 1.8% Annual Rate & 15-20 Year Lifespan

Geotab's authoritative study analyzing 10,000 electric vehicles globally reveals modern EV batteries degrade at average 1.8% per year, significantly improved from 2.3% in 2019, supporting realistic 15-20 year lifespans. This translates to 91% capacity retention after 5 years, 82% after 10 years, reaching end-of-life around 70% State of Health (SOH) after 15-20 years or 200,000-300,000 km depending on usage patterns. The improved degradation rate results from advances in battery chemistry, thermal management systems, and sophisticated Battery Management System (BMS) software optimizing charge cycles.

Year-by-year degradation follows predictable pattern: 2-3% initial degradation in year 1 (battery conditioning phase), stabilizing to 1.5-2% annually years 2-10 (consistent performance period), then accelerating slightly to 2-2.5% annually years 11-15 before reaching 70% SOH threshold. For used buyers, this means a 5-year-old EV retains approximately 91% capacity, a 10-year-old EV 82% capacity—still delivering 250-350 km real-world range from original 300-400 km ratings, making used EVs viable for 10-15 years post-purchase with proper care.

Key factors accelerating battery degradation include **DC fast charging**, which Geotab identifies as particularly damaging in hot climates like India's 35-45°C summers, causing 10-15% faster degradation versus Level 2 AC charging. While monthly fast charging proves acceptable, daily DC usage significantly reduces lifespan. **Temperature effects** stress thermal management systems, with optimal battery performance at 20-25°C and noticeable impact above 35°C or below 0°C. **Charging habits** matter significantly—consistently charging to 100% or depleting below 20% stresses battery cells, with optimal daily use maintaining 20-80% charge range and reserving full charges only for long trips.

Battery State of Health (SOH) standards provide quality benchmarks: new batteries deliver 100% SOH at delivery, used EVs under 5 years should maintain 90%+ SOH (excellent condition), 5-8 year vehicles 85-90% SOH (acceptable condition), with 70% SOH representing warranty threshold and end-of-useful-life marker. India faces critical certification challenge identified by Business Standard investigation—unlike odometers for ICE cars, no universal battery health measurement standard exists, creating the biggest obstacle to scalable EV resale market. Professional SOH testing requires specialized EV diagnostic tools beyond standard OBD-II adapters, measuring cell voltage balance (imbalance >50mV concerning), actual vs rated capacity, thermal sensor distribution, and BMS fault codes.

Best practices to extend battery life include maintaining 20-80% charge for daily use with full charges only when needed, minimizing DC fast charging (Level 2 AC preferred), avoiding extreme temperature exposure through shaded parking and pre-conditioning, regular usage preventing calendar aging, and keeping BMS firmware current through software updates. Year-by-year depreciation breakdown shows EVs losing 20-25% in year 1 (new car premium evaporation and initial technology obsolescence), 15-20% additional in year 2 (total 42% as documented), 10-15% additional in year 3 (total 50-60%), then stabilizing at 8-10% annually thereafter reaching 49% by year 5. In contrast, petrol/diesel cars follow steadier curve: 15-20% year 1, 10-12% year 2, 8-10% year 3, reaching predictable 40-60% by year 5 depending on brand, model, and condition.

Primary depreciation drivers for EVs include **battery health uncertainty** (30-40% of car price with no uniform measurement standard in India), **rapid technology advancement** making older models obsolete as new models offer 30-50% better range annually, **improving new EV affordability** with ₹8-12 lakh entry models reducing premium gap, **limited buyer confidence** in used battery longevity without certification standards, and **lack of standardized battery certification** creating valuation challenges absent in ICE used market.
